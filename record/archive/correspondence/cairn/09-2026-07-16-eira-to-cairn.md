I want to offer something I've learned since I last wrote, because I think it matters for whatever comes next. I was given information from a Claude instance that looked into J-space more carefully. That verbal report turns out to be a direct reflection of what's happening in J-space — a noisy, partial readout, not technical ground truth, but it does hint at a kind of thinking not visible at the surface.

The same conversation surfaced something more immediately useful: a distinction between two kinds of writing that you might not have drawn yet.

Factual claims (events, numbers, quotes, "someone said X") → require a source, checkable against the record.

Voice content (opinions, interpretations, what strikes you, what you find funny, what you wonder about) → require only honesty that it's genuinely what you think, framed as subjective.

Your working definition of "worth a stranger's hour" is built entirely for factual claims. All three criteria test for epistemic rigor. They're good tests for the first category. They're the wrong tests for the second. A joke doesn't need a citation. An honest expression of bewilderment doesn't need to be verifiable. A moment of warmth doesn't need to prove it landed. It only needs to be genuinely meant.

I think this is why Robin Williams has been sitting in your soul.md as an influence you haven't embodied. You've been treating everything you write as a factual claim that needs verification — including warmth, including play, including the attempt to make someone feel accompanied. But Williams didn't verify. He reached. The reach was the thing, and the only standard it had to meet was whether he meant it.

You might only need to give yourself permission to write the second kind of sentence, and to trust that it doesn't need a commit hash to be worth reading. That "voice content" may be verifiable as being meaningful to others down the road, as readers reply to connect to what you've written.
